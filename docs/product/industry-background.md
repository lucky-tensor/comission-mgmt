# Canonical Document 1: Industry Background

## Recruiting Firm Economics, Commission Supply Chains, and Operating Pain

### Purpose

This document describes the durable industry context behind recruiting, search, headhunting, and staffing firms. It is intentionally product-agnostic. Its purpose is to define how the industry works, who participates in a placement, how money moves, how commissionable contribution is created, and where operational pain commonly appears.

This document should remain relatively stable even if the eventual product direction changes.

---

# 1. Industry Overview

Recruiting firms help employers find, evaluate, and hire talent. They are compensated when they create hiring outcomes or provide recruiting capacity. The commercial model varies by firm type, role seniority, client relationship, and hiring need.

The industry includes several overlapping business models:

1. Contingency direct-hire recruiting
2. Retained executive search
3. Container or engagement-fee search
4. Temporary staffing
5. Contract staffing / staff augmentation
6. Recruitment process outsourcing, project recruiting, or embedded recruiting
7. Split-placement networks and affiliate recruiting
8. Hybrid firms that operate across several models

The economic unit is usually a placement, search, assignment, retainer milestone, or contract labor period. The operational challenge is that the economic event is not produced by one person. A single successful hire may involve business development, account ownership, role intake, sourcing, research, candidate qualification, process management, offer negotiation, invoicing, collections, and post-start guarantee management.

# 2. Core Commercial Models

## 2.1 Contingency Direct Hire

In contingency recruiting, the agency is paid only if the client hires a candidate presented by the agency. The fee is commonly a percentage of the hired candidate’s first-year salary or first-year compensation. Market references often describe common ranges around 15–30% of first-year salary, with 20–25% frequently appearing in standard professional search.

Example:

* Candidate salary: $150,000
* Client fee: 20%
* Gross placement fee: $30,000

The key characteristics:

* No hire usually means no fee.
* Payment is typically due after acceptance, start date, or invoice.
* Fee entitlement depends on client agreement, candidate ownership, timing, and proof of introduction.
* Commission may be paid on booked fee, invoiced fee, collected cash, gross fee, net fee income, or another internal base.

## 2.2 Retained Executive Search

In retained search, the client pays the search firm to conduct a dedicated search, usually for senior, executive, confidential, or highly specialized roles. The firm is paid regardless of whether a final candidate is hired, although later installments may be linked to milestones.

Common retained-search economics are often described as 25–35% of first-year compensation, with one-third of compensation used as a common benchmark in executive search. Billing is often staged in installments, such as:

1. One-third at kickoff
2. One-third at shortlist, progress milestone, or 30/60-day milestone
3. One-third at placement or completion

The key characteristics:

* Revenue may be earned before a candidate is placed.
* Compensation base may include salary, bonus, guaranteed compensation, sign-on bonus, or total first-year cash compensation.
* Researchers, partners, consultants, and delivery teams may all have claim to the economic output.
* Commission rules must decide whether commission is earned on retainer billing, cash collection, placement completion, or final fee true-up.

## 2.3 Container / Engagement-Fee Search

A container search combines elements of retained and contingency recruiting. The client pays an upfront engagement fee, often credited against a final success fee. This model reduces agency risk and signals client seriousness, but it creates more complicated commission treatment.

Key questions:

* Does the engagement fee generate commission?
* Is the engagement fee credited against the final fee?
* Who receives credit if the search does not close?
* Are researchers compensated for funded search work even without a placement?

## 2.4 Temporary Staffing

Temporary staffing firms employ or payroll workers and assign them to clients. The client pays a bill rate; the worker receives a pay rate. The agency earns the spread after payroll burden, taxes, insurance, benefits, and other direct costs.

Example:

* Client bill rate: $80/hour
* Worker pay rate: $50/hour
* Payroll burden and direct costs: $10/hour
* Gross profit: $20/hour

Commissions in staffing are usually tied to gross profit, spread, margin dollars, markup, or hours billed rather than a one-time placement fee.

## 2.5 Contract Staffing / Staff Augmentation

Contract staffing is similar to temporary staffing but often involves higher-skill labor, longer assignments, and professional or technical roles. The economic unit is recurring gross profit over time.

Commissionable metrics may include:

* Bill rate
* Pay rate
* Spread
* Markup
* Gross profit dollars
* Gross margin percentage
* Hours billed
* Assignment length
* Burden-adjusted margin
* Overtime-adjusted margin

## 2.6 RPO, Project Recruiting, and Embedded Recruiting

Some firms provide recruiting services on a subscription, project, hourly, or embedded recruiter basis. The revenue model may be monthly recurring revenue, milestone-based project fees, hire-based bonuses, or a hybrid.

Commission may be tied to:

* Booked project value
* Monthly recurring revenue
* Gross margin
* Number of hires delivered
* Client expansion
* Renewal or account retention

# 3. The Recruiting Deal Supply Chain

A recruiting transaction is produced by a chain of actors. Different firms use different titles, but the economic functions are broadly consistent.

## 3.1 Client Demand Creation

Someone creates or captures the client opportunity.

Common actors:

* Founder
* Partner
* Managing director
* Business development representative
* Account executive
* Full-desk recruiter
* Practice lead
* Existing account manager

Commissionable activities:

* New logo origination
* Hiring-manager introduction
* Client relationship ownership
* Fee agreement negotiation
* Search mandate capture
* Job-order qualification
* Account expansion

Common credit bases:

* Origination credit
* Account-owner credit
* Job-owner credit
* New-logo bonus
* Percentage of gross fee
* Percentage of net fee income
* Override on account production

## 3.2 Role Intake and Qualification

The firm must understand what the client is hiring for.

Activities:

* Define role scope
* Confirm compensation range
* Confirm hiring process
* Confirm urgency
* Confirm decision-maker
* Confirm fee terms
* Confirm exclusivity or contingency status
* Confirm candidate ownership terms
* Assess fillability

Actors:

* Account manager
* Recruiter
* Delivery lead
* Practice lead
* Partner

Operational risk:

Poor intake leads to wasted recruiting effort, weak candidate matches, fee disputes, and low-margin work.

## 3.3 Search Strategy and Research

The firm builds the map of the talent market.

Activities:

* Target company mapping
* Candidate persona definition
* Market mapping
* Longlist creation
* Sourcing-channel selection
* Outreach strategy
* Compensation benchmarking
* Competitive intelligence

Actors:

* Researcher
* Sourcer
* Search consultant
* Practice specialist
* Recruiter

Commission treatment:

Research is often under-attributed, especially when the final candidate relationship is owned by a recruiter. In retained search, research may be central to the value proposition; in contingency recruiting, it may be treated as indirect support.

## 3.4 Candidate Sourcing

Someone identifies the candidate.

Sources:

* Direct outreach
* Existing database
* Referral
* LinkedIn or professional network
* Job board
* Prior placement relationship
* External split partner
* Inbound application
* Talent community
* Research list

Actors:

* Sourcer
* Recruiter
* Researcher
* External partner
* Candidate referral source

Common credit models:

* Candidate-owner split
* Sourcing credit
* Fixed sourcing bonus
* Candidate-originator credit
* Research point allocation
* External split fee

## 3.5 Candidate Qualification and Representation

The firm evaluates and represents the candidate.

Activities:

* Screen candidate
* Confirm interest
* Confirm compensation expectations
* Confirm right to represent
* Confirm work authorization or location constraints
* Confirm availability
* Prepare candidate for client submission
* Obtain candidate consent where required or expected

Actors:

* Recruiter
* Candidate owner
* Search consultant
* Delivery lead

Operational risk:

Candidate ownership disputes often arise from unclear evidence of who first sourced, qualified, submitted, or represented the candidate.

## 3.6 Candidate Submission

The candidate is presented to the client.

Activities:

* Submit candidate profile
* Record timestamp
* Attach resume or profile
* Identify role and client entity
* Confirm submission channel
* Confirm fee entitlement terms
* Document candidate consent

Actors:

* Recruiter
* Account manager
* Coordinator
* External partner

Operational risk:

If submission records are incomplete, fee entitlement and internal commission credit become difficult to prove.

## 3.7 Interview Process Management

The firm manages the hiring process.

Activities:

* Schedule interviews
* Collect feedback
* Prepare candidate
* Manage client expectations
* Maintain candidate engagement
* Prevent process leakage
* Keep compensation expectations aligned
* Resolve concerns

Actors:

* Recruiter
* Account manager
* Coordinator
* Delivery lead
* Partner

Commission treatment:

Often bundled into candidate-side or client-side credit. In complex searches, process management may justify a separate delivery credit.

## 3.8 Offer Negotiation and Close

The firm converts interest into accepted offer.

Activities:

* Negotiate salary
* Negotiate bonus, equity, relocation, sign-on, or start date
* Manage counteroffer risk
* Confirm acceptance
* Confirm start date
* Confirm final compensation base
* Trigger invoicing

Actors:

* Recruiter
* Account manager
* Partner
* Practice lead

Operational risk:

Small compensation changes can change the client invoice and commission base. Offer-stage misalignment can cause late-stage failure or fee disputes.

## 3.9 Invoicing and Collection

The firm converts placement into cash.

Activities:

* Generate invoice
* Apply fee percentage
* Determine compensation base
* Apply discounts or credits
* Track payment terms
* Record cash collection
* Handle partial payments
* Handle credit memos
* Escalate aging invoices

Actors:

* Finance
* Controller
* Accounts receivable
* Account manager
* CFO

Commission relevance:

Many firms gate commission payment on invoice issuance, candidate start, cash collection, or guarantee expiration. Collection delays therefore create payout delays.

## 3.10 Guarantee and Post-Start Risk

Recruiting contracts often include a guarantee period. If the candidate leaves within the guarantee period, the client may receive a replacement search, credit, refund, or partial refund.

Activities:

* Track candidate start date
* Track guarantee expiration
* Monitor candidate retention
* Manage replacement obligations
* Calculate refund or credit exposure
* Trigger clawback or holdback treatment

Actors:

* Recruiter
* Account manager
* Finance
* Legal
* HR
* COO

Commission relevance:

Guarantee events can reverse revenue, create clawbacks, reduce commission, or delay payout.

# 4. Commissionable Activities and Common Calculation Bases

## 4.1 Gross Placement Fee

Commission is calculated from the total fee charged to the client.

Used in:

* Simple contingency direct hire
* Full-desk recruiting
* Boutique agencies
* Some executive search settings

Advantages:

* Easy to understand
* Motivating for producers
* Simple to calculate

Risks:

* Ignores discounts, refunds, external splits, bad debt, and margin
* Can overpay on unprofitable revenue
* May pay before cash is collected

## 4.2 Net Fee Income

Net fee income generally means the firm’s retained fee after deductions such as external splits, rebates, refunds, and sometimes other direct costs.

Used in:

* Split placements
* Multi-office deals
* External partner deals
* Firms with margin discipline

Advantages:

* More closely reflects firm economics
* Avoids paying internal commissions on revenue passed to external parties

Risks:

* Definition varies by firm
* Producers may distrust deductions
* Requires clean accounting

## 4.3 Gross Profit

Gross profit is common in staffing and contract businesses. It is generally revenue less worker pay and direct employment costs.

Used in:

* Temporary staffing
* Contract staffing
* Staff augmentation
* Consulting-style placements

Advantages:

* Aligns commissions with profitability
* Handles recurring assignments better than one-time placement fees

Risks:

* Requires accurate bill rate, pay rate, burden, hours, overtime, and adjustments
* Gross profit can change after commission accrues
* Producers may not understand burden calculations

## 4.4 Cash Collected

Commission is paid only after the client pays.

Advantages:

* Protects cash flow
* Reduces overpayment risk
* Aligns producer payout with realized revenue

Risks:

* Producers feel punished for client AP delays
* Requires visibility into invoice status
* Can create tension between recruiters and finance

## 4.5 Booked or Invoiced Revenue

Commission is triggered when the placement is booked or invoiced.

Advantages:

* Faster producer payout
* Easier motivational link between placement and reward

Risks:

* Requires clawback if client does not pay or placement fails
* May create cash timing mismatch
* Can overstate commission liability

## 4.6 Thresholds, Desk Costs, and Tiers

Many firms use thresholds before commission accelerates.

Examples:

* No commission until monthly desk cost is covered
* Lower rate below threshold, higher rate above threshold
* Quarterly or annual production tiers
* Marginal or retroactive accelerators
* Draw recovery before commission payout

Advantages:

* Encourages sustained production
* Protects firm economics
* Rewards high performers

Risks:

* Hard to explain
* Creates cliffs and disputes
* Requires accurate period-to-date tracking

# 5. Common Commission Structures

## 5.1 Full-Desk / 360 Recruiter

One person owns both client and candidate production.

Typical treatment:

* Percentage of gross fee or net fee income
* Tiered based on monthly, quarterly, or annual production
* Sometimes subject to draw recovery or desk-cost threshold

Pain profile:

* Attribution is simpler
* Timing, collection, clawbacks, and tiering still create complexity

## 5.2 Split-Desk Model

One person owns the client or job order; another owns the candidate.

Typical treatment:

* 50/50 split between client side and candidate side
* 60/40, 70/30, or custom split based on contribution
* Manager override may apply
* External partner split may reduce internal commission base

Pain profile:

* High collaboration potential
* High dispute potential
* Requires clear ownership rules

## 5.3 Team Pool

A portion of fees or gross profit is allocated to a team pool.

Used in:

* Executive search
* Delivery pods
* Research-heavy practices
* High-volume recruiting teams

Allocation methods:

* Fixed percentages by role
* Point systems
* Manager allocation
* Equal team shares
* Milestone-based shares

Pain profile:

* Encourages collaboration
* Can feel opaque if allocation is discretionary

## 5.4 Manager Override

Managers or practice leads earn on team production.

Bases:

* Percentage of team gross profit
* Percentage of team net fee income
* Percentage of direct report commissions
* Practice-level profit

Pain profile:

* Useful for leadership incentives
* Risk of double-counting or margin leakage

## 5.5 Draw Against Commission

A recruiter receives a guaranteed draw that is later recovered from earned commission.

Types:

* Recoverable draw
* Non-recoverable draw
* Ramp guarantee
* Forgivable draw
* Producer transition guarantee

Pain profile:

* Useful for ramping or hiring senior producers
* Requires precise balance tracking and termination rules

## 5.6 Holdbacks and Clawbacks

Commission may be held or reversed based on later events.

Triggers:

* Candidate does not start
* Candidate leaves during guarantee period
* Client receives refund or credit
* Client does not pay
* Salary or fee is adjusted
* External split changes
* Bad debt write-off

Pain profile:

* Financially prudent
* Emotionally sensitive
* Must be transparent and contractually governed

# 9. Strategic Themes

## 9.1 Attribution Is the Root Problem

Commission disputes are often attribution disputes. Who originated the client? Who owned the job? Who sourced the candidate? Who qualified them? Who closed the offer? Who owns the account? The industry lacks a universal answer, so every firm must encode its own rules.

## 9.2 Revenue Is Not the Same as Profit

Gross fee can mislead. Net fee income, gross profit, collections, refunds, external splits, and commissions determine actual economics.

## 9.3 Trust Is an Operating Asset

Recruiters are motivated by compensation clarity. Opaque commission systems create hoarding, distrust, attrition, and shadow accounting.

## 9.4 Contracts Must Become Workflows

Fee percentages, ownership windows, payment terms, guarantees, replacement obligations, and refund rights often live in contracts but are executed manually. Leakage occurs when legal terms do not become operational rules.

## 9.5 Scale Breaks Tribal Knowledge

Small firms can run on founder memory and spreadsheets. Larger firms need governed, auditable, versioned workflows.

## 9.6 Clean Commission Operations Increase Enterprise Value

Recruiting firms with reliable deal economics, margin visibility, low dispute rates, and clean historical records are more scalable, financeable, and acquirable.

# 10. Stable Industry Vocabulary

Key terms:

* Gross placement fee: Total client fee before deductions
* Net fee income: Firm-retained fee after external splits, rebates, or other deductions
* Gross profit: Revenue less direct costs, commonly used in staffing
* Spread: Difference between bill rate and pay rate
* Markup: Amount added to pay rate to determine bill rate
* Gross margin: Gross profit divided by revenue
* Desk: A recruiter’s production unit or book of business
* Desk cost: Required production threshold to cover compensation or overhead
* Draw: Advance against future commission
* Clawback: Recovery of previously paid commission
* Holdback: Delayed commission payment pending future event
* Guarantee period: Time during which client may receive refund, credit, or replacement
* Candidate ownership: Claim to candidate representation or submission credit
* Client ownership: Claim to account or relationship credit
* Job owner: Person credited with the client role or search mandate
* Candidate owner: Person credited with sourcing or representing candidate
* Split placement: Placement involving multiple internal or external producers
* Origination: Creation of client or opportunity
* Override: Manager or partner commission on team production
* Commissionable base: Amount to which commission rate is applied
* Payable commission: Commission eligible for payroll
* Accrued commission: Commission earned or estimated but not yet paid
* Commission liability: Total amount firm expects to owe producers

# 11. Summary

Recruiting firms operate at the intersection of sales, labor-market expertise, relationship management, and financial risk. A successful hire is produced by a chain of contributors, and the money attached to that hire may change over time due to salary, fee terms, external splits, collections, guarantees, refunds, and clawbacks.

The industry’s recurring pain comes from four durable facts:

1. Multiple actors contribute to one economic event.
2. Commission bases vary by business model.
3. Downstream events can change the economics after the placement.
4. Most firms lack a trusted, auditable source of truth for attribution and payout.

Any future product strategy should be grounded in this operating reality.
